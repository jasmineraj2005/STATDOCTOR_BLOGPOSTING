## How does the NSW locum system work?

NSW's locum system is structured around 15 Local Health Districts (LHDs), each responsible for its own staffing and locum engagement. This decentralised approach means that when you contract as a locum, you're dealing with a specific LHD rather than NSW Health as a whole. This distinction is important because each LHD may have slightly different rates, compliance requirements, and onboarding processes.

The engagement of locum medical officers in NSW is regulated by the [NSW Health Policy Directive PD2019_006](https://www.health.nsw.gov.au/business/locums/Pages/default.aspx), which outlines conditions for locum engagement, including credentialing and supervision requirements. Additionally, NSW Health maintains a [Register of Medical Locum Agencies](https://www.health.nsw.gov.au/business/locums/Pages/locum-register.aspx), ensuring agencies meet specific standards before placing locums in public hospitals.

Locums can also source shifts directly through LHD staffing units, particularly for senior roles, which might avoid agency fees but require more effort. Some hospitals even list shifts on their internal portals, allowing for direct engagement.

## What are the locum pay rates in NSW?

Locum pay rates in NSW vary significantly depending on the role, level of training, location, and time of day. For Resident Medical Officers (RMOs), the average rate is A$139.71/hr, with night shifts offering higher pay at A$160.16/hr compared to A$137.85/hr for day shifts, according to [Medlo](https://medlo.com.au/doctor-salary-australia/resident). This reflects the increased demand and compensation for less desirable hours.

Emergency Medicine roles in metropolitan areas like Gosford typically offer around A$100/hr, while ICU registrar roles in regional centres such as Dubbo range from A$140 to A$160/hr. Regional areas often provide higher rates due to workforce shortages.

For GP ED roles, regional NSW is particularly lucrative, with day rates ranging from A$2,000 to A$3,000, depending on the location. Anaesthetics roles command the highest rates, with listings showing up to A$6,000/day in Windsor, highlighting the demand in regional and peri-urban areas.

## Where can locum doctors find work in NSW?

NSW's locum market is split between metropolitan Sydney and regional areas. Metro Sydney offers a high volume of shifts with shorter commutes but lower rates due to higher competition. This market suits doctors seeking regular shifts without the need for relocation, with opportunities in teaching hospitals and major trauma centres.

In contrast, regional NSW provides greater financial incentives and a higher demand for locum doctors. Key towns like Dubbo, Broken Hill, and Moree regularly advertise locum roles across various specialties, including ICU, general medicine, and GP ED. These areas often cover travel and accommodation, significantly impacting your net earnings.

## What are the key differences from other states?

Locum work in NSW differs from other states like Victoria, which operates a more centralised model through HealthShare Victoria. In NSW, the LHD structure means shifts are not centrally registered, requiring engagement with multiple agencies or LHD staffing units to access the full range of opportunities.

NSW's formal agency register, which includes certification requirements, provides quality assurance but limits unlisted agencies from accessing public hospital contracts. This differs from Victoria, where such a register is not as formalised. While rates are broadly comparable between NSW and Victoria, NSW's regional market is larger and more diverse, reflecting its geography and population distribution.

## How to get started as a locum in NSW?

Starting as a locum in NSW involves several steps, similar to other Australian states but with some NSW-specific requirements. First, ensure you have current and unrestricted [AHPRA registration](https://www.ahpra.gov.au/). An Australian Business Number (ABN) is necessary for contractor-based arrangements, which are common in NSW. If you don't have an ABN, refer to our guide on [getting an ABN as a locum doctor](/post/getting-an-abn-as-a-locum-doctor-a-step-by-step-guide).

Medical indemnity insurance is critical, covering NSW public hospitals and specific role types. Each LHD has its own credentialing process, so prepare to provide your CV, proof of registration, indemnity certificate, and references. Start this process early, as it can take 1–2 weeks.

Register with agencies on the NSW Health register or contact LHD staffing units directly. Agencies often simplify the process by handling credentialing and logistics, making them a good starting point for new locums.
